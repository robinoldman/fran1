Narendra Modi's punjab problem

This article discusses the recent security crackdown in Punjab, India, initiated by Prime Minister Narendra Modi's government. Francis suggests that the intense response, including a statewide manhunt for a Sikh separatist, might be a politically motivated move to manufacture a security situation for electoral gains in the upcoming 2024 elections. The crackdown is viewed as a diversionary tactic by Modi, deflecting attention from unresolved crises in Punjab and India while creating a perception of a renewed threat of Sikh separatism.

After the coup

Here, Wade discusses the aftermath of Myanmar's 2021 military coup, outlining the brutal suppression by the junta, the formation of the National Unity Government (NUG), and the rise of armed resistance by People's Defence Force (PDF) battalions and ethnic groups. The conflict has led to extensive violence, displacement, and a growing death toll. It delves into Myanmar's military history, economic interests, and diverse resistance movements. Despite international sanctions, the military's aggressive stance indicates dim prospects for a peaceful resolution.

Edge Plain border

Wade explores the impact of borders in a changing climate, questioning their permanence and role in environmental policies, referencing the National Rally party's eco-nationalism. James Crawford's book, "The Edge of the Plain," argues against the idea of borders as enduring and highlights instances, such as the Graffener Glacier, where borders shift due to climate change, challenging traditional notions. Every day peace

Wade examines the often-overlooked phenomenon of "everyday resistance" during times of conflict, drawing on examples from Nazi Germany and Rwanda, where small acts of nonconformity challenged the pervasive violence. Roger Mac Ginty's book, "Everyday Peace," delves into the subfield of peace and conflict studies, exploring how localized, bottom-up acts of solidarity, tolerance, and noncompliance, collectively termed "everyday peace," can disrupt the divisive logic of conflict and contribute to healing in fractured societies.

A State of ones own

The book "Neither Settler nor Native: The Making and Unmaking of Permanent Minorities" by Mahmood Mamdani argues that postcolonial violence in former European colonies is a continuation of colonial logic, asserting that genocides, ethnic cleansing, and population transfers serve similar ends to the violence deployed by European powers during colonization, challenging the notion that such violence represents a return to tribal ways and emphasizing the role of the nation-state model in perpetuating violence; the author explores historical cases from Native American extermination to the Nuremberg trials and examines the impact of these events on contemporary societies.

Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o and the Tyranny of Language

Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o reflects on the enduring impact of colonial education, particularly in Kenya's Alliance High School, where English was imposed as a symbol of power, rationality, and intelligence while local languages were denigrated. In his seminal work, "Decolonising the Mind," Ngũgĩ explores how colonial classrooms served as tools of psychological conquest, emphasizing the lasting consequences of language manipulation in colonized societies beyond Africa.

Journalist and novelist James Meek delves into Brexit's impact on Britain's socio-political fabric in "Dreams of Leaving and Remaining." Ghost Ships

Reports in late May revealed the discovery of nearly 140 graves in abandoned human trafficking camps near the Thailand-Malaysia border. These camps were sites of brutal violence and held trafficked refugees and migrants for extended periods. Despite previous revelations of such camps, the mass graves underscore the persistence and growth of human trafficking operations in the region, raising questions about the effectiveness of efforts to combat this illicit trade.
